#1 Manchester United’s crossing-maniac: David Moyes’ fault?
 
Since the Scottish coach David Moyes took charge of the English Premier League football team Manchester United from the retired legend Sir Alex Ferguson by the end of the 2012/13 season, the 13-time Premier League title winner has been widely criticized by media and fans for its outdated and unskillful crossing tactics on the pitch. In a home match against Fulham on February 9th, 2014, among a total of 85 crosses created by both teams in 90 minutes, Manchester United contributed an insane 81 crosses, none of which led to a goal. Fulham boss Rene Meulensteen described United's style as "straightforward". With a disappointing record of 13-6-8 in the 2013/14 season, Manchester United has now dropped to the sixth on the Premier League table.
 
Yet, in United’s 28 previous seasons led by Sir Alex Ferguson, the team was widely known for its massive use of wingers, which also involves long-ball crosses from both of the attacking sides on the pitch. I would like to visualize the actual change of Manchester United’s crossing tactics from the 2012/13 season to the current (2013/14) season and find out if David Moyes has indeed adopted more of the crossing tactics than his predecessor Sir Alex Ferguson.
 
Match data and statistics by Manchester United I need in order to make the project successful:
  • Total number of crosses per match;
  • Success rate of crosses (crosses that led to an attempt shot or a goal);
  • Number of crosses produced per game for each player;
  • Areas where players produced crosses. On October 15th, 2012, The Guardian published a similar interactive analysis of Manchester City's match data on one single Premier League match against Bolton Wanderers, using the Tableau software.
 
Sources:
  • My go-to source would be Opta Sports, world’s leading football data company. Despite the fact that its data services are only open to media partners, Opta Sports does have a new “playground” site that allows users to make data requests for academic, developing and blogging purposes. Once this project proposal is approved, I will submit my data request via the academic channel very soon.
  • Some other football match stats websites offer general in-match stats, but the detailed crossing stats cannot be found. They are listed here for back-up sources: http://www.redcafe.net/threads/useful-statistics-and-tactics-resources.331840/

#2 Rise and fall: How are the top national football teams doing since South Africa?
 
Every month, the FIFA Ranking page publishes an updated men’s national football team ranking. Prior to the 2014 Brazil World Cup, I would like to visualize the performances of the top 2010 World Cup teams from 2010 to 2014.
 
FIFA offers no download link to its data spreadsheet of the FIFA Ranking, but there is a scraping tool that will allow me to export all teams’ average ranking by year. Besides, there is an unofficial FIFA Ranking page that can be a backup source for me to try scrape the data.
 
The FIFA Ranking data I need in order to make the project successful:
  • Month-by-month ranking of each national football team;
  • Month-by-month FIFA Ranking points of each national football team;
  • Number of matches played by each national football team, and the overall match records.
 
If possible, I will be able to compare the gap between the actual record/performance and the according FIFA Ranking of some national football teams.
 
I will need to learn more about data-scraping skills and tools, as well as D3 skills.
 
I will pitch this project to news sites and online football forums, mostly those in England.
