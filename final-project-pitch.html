Manchester United’s crossing-maniac: David Moyes’ fault?

***

Since the Scottish coach David Moyes took charge of the English Premier League football team Manchester United from the retired legend Sir Alex Ferguson by the end of the 2012/13 season, the 13-time Premier League title winner has been widely criticized by media and supporters for its outdated and unskillful crossing tactics on the pitch. In a home match against Fulham on February 9th, 2014, among a total of 85 crosses created by both teams in 90 minutes, Manchester United contributed an insane 81 crosses, none of which led to a goal. Fulham boss Rene Meulensteen described United's style as "straightforward". With a disappointing record of 13-6-8 in the 2013/14 season, Manchester United has now dropped to the seventh on the Premier League table.

Yet, in United’s 28 previous seasons led by Sir Alex Ferguson, the team was widely known for its massive use of wingers, which also involves long-ball crosses from both of the attacking sides on the pitch. I would like to visualize the actual change of Manchester United’s crossing tactics from the 2012/13 season to the current (2013/14) season and find out if David Moyes has indeed adopted more of the crossing tactics in open play than his predecessor Sir Alex Ferguson.

I will need to acquire the match-by-match open play crossing stats of Manchester United in the 2012/13 and 2013/14 English Premier League seasons, in the following forms:

  • Which player produced the cross;
  • Exact position where the cross was made. On October 15th, 2012, The Guardian published a similar interactive analysis of Manchester City's match data on one single Premier League match against Bolton Wanderers, using the Tableau software;
  • Whether the cross was successful (either led to an attempt shot or a goal) or not;
  • Total number of open play crosses per match;
  • Overall crossing accuracy per match.
